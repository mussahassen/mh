Data Science to Me is...

There are many ways to model and further understand the systems of real-world problems using mathematical models.

The systems we may (not) see in our lives, from the growth of a species population, to effectiveness of a policy or an advertisement; or testing a new drug, can be modeled by ubiquitous mathematical/statistical frameworks. For example differential equations:

  • A model developed by Alfred Lotka and Vito Volterra in the 1920s, uses a pair of first-order nonlinear differential equations to describe how a predator and its prey, interact over time.
  • The Susceptible-Infected-Recovered model, developed by William Ogilvy Kermack and Anderson Gray McKendrick, is the cornerstone of modern epidemiology. It puts a population into three compartments. Then it uses equations to track how individuals move between them based on the infection and recovery rates.
  • Even a simpler example, regression: when testing a new drug, researchers use ANOVA to determine the relationship between the dosage and the physiological effect.

and so much more. How these functions and operations came about to model such "Deterministic Chaos" in these systems we often overlook, is what I'm intrigued by.
